Why this is a structurally weak base for freemium:
Freemium is financially viable when paid users have high enough ARPU that a small conversion rate (2–5%) generates substantial revenue to subsidise the free tier. Here, 58% of accounts pay only $10.15/mo on average — barely above the floor. There is almost no pricing headroom. Compared to a healthy freemium product (Starter ARPU of $30–50+), TS needs conversion rates 3× higher just to generate equivalent revenue per free user. The top 5.9% of accounts (6+ GBs) generate 37% of MRR at $33–99/mo — those are the accounts that could carry a free tier. The 1-GB majority cannot. Launching freemium here means the accounts you're trying to subsidise are almost as cheap as the free users you're trying to convert.

The structural problem in numbers: The blended ARPU of a converted Free user ($10.08) is nearly identical to what current 1-GB Starters pay ($10.15). A healthy freemium product has blended ARPU of $30–50+, meaning a 3% CR yields $0.90–$1.50 per free user per month. TS at 3% CR yields $0.30 per free user. TS needs 4× more free users than a typical freemium SaaS just to generate the same revenue per sign-up. The combined risk ($9,465/mo) is mathematically unreachable from subscription CR alone — the combined break-even requires 939 conversions/month, which at 10x sign-ups (1,150/mo) would demand a 81.6% CR. Store commission must carry the majority of the recovery.

Insights analysis
Critical
Your acquisition funnel is already dominated by the lowest-value segment — freemium exposes it, not fixes it
67% of new clients and 58% of existing accounts are 1-GB users paying median $8.25/mo. This is a structural problem that exists with or without a free plan. Freemium forces the question: is $0 + PLG upside better than $8.25 + the current slow-growth trajectory? The data shows the size of the risk, not the answer.
Critical
Free and Starter are dangerously close in delivered value — the gap is thin
Strip branding, cloning, translations, dynamic text, time-based access, analytics, and email templates and Free delivers ~80% of what Starter delivers to a passive 1-property host. A host who built their guide once and rarely edits it has no strong reason to pay $8.25/mo. The upgrade motion depends on multiple soft levers working simultaneously — an optimistic assumption for low-engagement users.
Critical
Free has chatbot. Starter doesn't. This breaks the upgrade ladder and orphans Starter
A Free user who hits the 3-chat/month cap and wants more will look at Starter ($8.25/mo), see zero chatbot, and skip directly to Growth ($11.58/mo) — or stay on Free. Starter is bypassed. Unless Starter gets a distinct chatbot feature, it has no compelling reason to exist between Free and Growth for any host who values AI automation.
Warning
75% of new 1-GB clients chose annual — the downgrade bomb detonates at renewal
86 of 115 new 1-GB clients last month paid upfront annual. They won't see the Free option until renewal. If Starter's value hasn't been made tangible through the year via weekly digests, cloning workflows, and 2nd-seat usage, the annual renewal moment becomes a concentrated mass-downgrade event. You have 12 months with each cohort to build retention habits — plan for it now.
Warning
Store commission model assumes Store usage — which you haven't validated for 1-GB accounts
The 20–25% commission is only a meaningful recovery lever if Free users actively transact. Low-volume 1-GB hosts often have too few guests to make upselling worthwhile. If the 1-GB Starter cohort has low Store adoption today, the commission mechanism generates near zero. Pull Store usage data by GB count before building financial recovery around this assumption.
Warning
TS Free being the best free guidebook on the market is also a market-distortion risk
Competitors charge $7–10/mo for products with fewer features than TS Free. Launching a materially better free product doesn't just attract new users — it signals to the entire market that guidebook software should be free, creating pressure on competitors to follow. Once the market shifts to freemium, recovering to paid-first is very difficult. This is a strategic one-way door, not a tactical experiment.
Strategic
The median GB count of 1 means Free formalises who you already serve — it's not a pivot, it's a reckoning
Half your customers have a single guidebook. Freemium doesn't create a new type of user — it removes the paywall for people who already represent your majority profile. The question isn't whether to serve 1-GB hosts. You already do. The question is whether $0 + commission + PLG growth velocity beats $8.25 + current churn + stagnant acquisition. That is a bet on growth rate, not on product-market fit.

Dangers and pitfalls
High severity
HIGH
Annual renewal is a concentrated downgrade event waiting to happen 75% of new 1-GB clients chose annual. In 12 months, every one of them sees the Free option at renewal. If Starter's value hasn't been made tangible through the year, renewals become a mass-downgrade moment hitting all at once rather than spread across months.
HIGH
Starter is the orphan tier — chatbot on Free, not on Starter Any Free user who wants more chatbot skips Starter entirely and goes to Growth. A tier that exists only as a price point — not a distinct value point — will have chronically low CR. Starter needs either chatbot lite or another unambiguous differentiator to justify its position.
HIGH
No Store usage data by tier — commission recovery is a blind assumption If 1-GB Starter accounts rarely use the Store today (plausible for low-volume hosts), the 20–25% commission generates near-zero revenue and the financial recovery model is missing a critical input.
HIGH
Freemium is a strategic one-way door — it resets market price expectations permanently Once TS offers a free plan, competitors face pressure to follow. Once the market normalises free guidebooks, recovering to paid-first pricing is extremely difficult. This is not a reversible experiment — it is a permanent repositioning of what the market believes guidebook software should cost.
Medium severity
MED
Passive hosts — the exact at-risk cohort — feel almost none of the upgrade levers AI prompt caps, cloning, analytics, and template limits are only felt by active hosts who revisit and refine their guide. A host who set up their guide once and sends emails occasionally hits zero friction on Free. For this cohort, the only real levers are the TS badge, the 2nd seat, and the 1-GB cap — and only one of those (the 2nd seat) is triggered by normal usage.
MED
The 10x sign-up assumption is unvalidated for a niche B2B product Consumer apps reliably see volume multipliers from freemium. B2B niche SaaS in a specific vertical (STR) is different — hosts discover tools through PMS integrations and community recommendations, not organic search virality. Hostfully has had a free tier for years and is not known for explosive PLG growth as a result.
MED
Guide cloning doesn't exist yet — a key Starter lever is missing at launch One of the strongest pull levers for Starter (guidebook duplication) is not currently built. Launching Free without it weakens the Starter upgrade story from day one. Hosts who would feel the gap can't feel it if the feature doesn't exist on Starter either.
MED
Free support cost is real and unbudgeted Potentially 5,000+ Free users generating even 5% support volume per quarter = 250+ tickets at $0 revenue. A Free-tier support policy (community-first, async-only, strict 48h cap) must be defined before launch — not after CS is overwhelmed with zero-revenue accounts.
MED
Template library UX is a conversion decision, not a design decision Showing 2 accessible templates with the rest visibly greyed and locked creates upgrade motivation. Hiding the locked templates creates nothing. If the implementation team hides rather than shows locked templates, this entire lever disappears silently without anyone noticing.
Bottom line
DimensionAssessment
Near-term MRR risk −$9,465/month at realistic assumptions (new MRR loss $1,118 + 15% existing downgrade $8,347). Annualised: −$113,580 before any PLG upside materialises. This is real money leaving before the growth bet pays off.
Break-even timeline At optimistic 10x sign-ups and 3% CR, recovery takes 12–18 months. At realistic 5x sign-ups and 2% CR, the model never breaks even on new MRR alone — Store commission must carry the gap.
What must be true for this to work Three things must all be true simultaneously: (1) Free drives significantly more top-of-funnel volume, (2) a meaningful share of Free users convert to Starter within 90 days, (3) Free users transact through the Store at a rate that generates measurable commission. If any one of these fails, the model is loss-making.
Starter tier health Starter currently has no chatbot (Free does), no analytics (locked to Growth+), and two unbuilt levers (cloning, version history). Before launching Free, Starter needs to be a genuinely compelling step up — not just a price tier with a slightly better support SLA and a second seat.
Market risk Launching the best free guidebook on the market permanently resets price expectations in the STR software segment. This is a one-way strategic door. Competitors will respond. The market will normalise free. Do this only if you are confident in the PLG playbook — not as an experiment you can reverse.
Three pre-launch requirements ① Build guide cloning on Starter — the strongest pull lever that doesn't yet exist.   ② Pull Store usage data by GB count — validate that commission recovery is real, not theoretical.   ③ Give Starter a chatbot feature — even 20 resolved/month — so there is a clear, product-level reason to upgrade from Free to Starter rather than going straight to Growth.
